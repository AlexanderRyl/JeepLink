Problem Analysis

Systemic Barriers to Modernization

The Philippine public transport ecosystem is navigating a precarious transition. Our research identifies four foundational pillars of dysfunction that necessitate a structural disruption.

Executive Summary

The "Problem" in Baguio is not just volume, but physics the interaction between antiquated machinery and unforgiving terrain. The current boundary system institutionalizes hazardous driving, while manual fare collection prevents the financial transparency needed for bank loans. Incremental changes have failed, a digital intervention is required to address both human and mechanical inefficiencies.

The Boundary System

Drivers pay a fixed "boundary" fee to operators, transferring 100% of market risk to the driver. This "hand-to-mouth" existence forces a perpetual hunt for passengers, leading to aggressive driving and chronic fatigue.

On-Demand Stopping

The flexibility of loading anywhere acts as a chaotic variable in traffic flow. A single stop in Baguio's narrow lanes blocks 50-100% of throughput, reducing average CBD speeds to just 9 km/h.

Fragmented Franchising

Baguio has 116 distinct lines and over 4,208 units. This fragmentation prevents centralized dispatching, leading to "bunching" on main arteries and underservice in peripheral areas.

Manual Fare Collection

The "Kaliwaan" system diverts driver attention and creates revenue leakage. Crucially, the lack of digital records makes the sector "unbankable," blocking access to loans for modern units.

Local Context

The Baguio "Bowl Effect"

Baguio’s topography acts as a multiplier for emissions. Heavy loads on steep ascents result in incomplete combustion from aging diesel engines. The city's "bowl" shape traps these pollutants, causing transport to account for 94% of the city's air pollutants. Furthermore, the rugged terrain creates "GPS Shadows," challenging standard tracking solutions.
